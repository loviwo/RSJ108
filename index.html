Journalism for Chismosas

Time: Right now!
Slay rating: 10/10

If you feel guilty when gossiping, you're doing it wrong. Yep—you heard me! It's possible to gossip in a guilt-free, ethical way, and I will teach you how to do this by highlighting the six mistakes you might be making while gossiping, and how you can fix them by applying some journalistic standards and ethical principles.

  • Mistakes

  • You're not reporting credible information
  • You're not citing your sources
  • You're not incorporating relevant quotes
  • You're not going above and beyond in your journalism
  • You're not taking journalism classes

  1. You're not reporting credible information

    The one thing that separates a good journalist from a bad journalist is trust. The same thing goes for chismosas—if you’re just spreading every rumor you heard from the grapevine, regardless of its truth, your information will not only be morally questionable, but factually questionable, too. The most scandalous and intriguing gossip usually has some element of truth to it, and if you want to inform and amuse your friends and contribute to the conversation, make sure you’re spreading (mostly) factual information.

  2. You're not citing your sources

    As a journalist, you can’t have all the information you write or report on background. If you do this, it’s just going to seem like you made it all up, and that’s not a good look. To be a credible reporter who knows what's up, debes que cite your sources and where you got your information from. You don’t have to provide an author and a date in parentheses, though—journalists use AP style! Simply attribute your information to a source within the structure of a sentence. Citing your sources when necessary builds trust and credibility. Did I mention that attributing your information to who you got it from also adds a layer of drama into your chisme? Cite your sources if you want to really spice things up.

  3. You're not incorporating relevant quotes

    As in any engaging article, podcast, or TV broadcast, quotes serve to increase the quality, readability, and (surprise surprise) credibility of the information you’re sharing. There are several different kinds of quotes you can incorporate into your gossip, and if you want to learn about the different kinds of quotes you can use in your journalism—I mean, gossip—click on this link to find out more.

  4. You're not going above and beyond in your journalism

    Are you committing to disseminating factual and interesting information as a journalist and a chismosa alike? If so, find the motivation to get out there and do some “journalism,” whether you’re reporting to your best friend or to the New York Times. Collect evidence, conduct interviews, and do your research. If the tea is piping hot and delicious, find out who’s pouring it.

  5. You're not taking journalism classes

    If you want to learn how to perfect your gossiping craft, taking a journalism class is a good way to start. You learn about all the different things I showed you in this tutorial, including how to establish credibility with your audience, how to attribute sources, and why it’s so important to know how to collect and report information that will keep the conversation going. Journalism also teaches you how to determine whether or not information can be trusted by teaching you how to be media literate. This is especially useful for learning how to gossip ethically and journalistically, because by staying informed and using critical thinking, you can find out which rumors are worth spreading, and which ones belong in the trash with yesterday's paper.
